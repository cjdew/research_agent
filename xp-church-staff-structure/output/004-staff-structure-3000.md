# Staff Structure at 3000 Attendance: What Changes from 2000

*This report builds on [001-church-staff-structure.md](001-church-staff-structure.md), [002-staff-structure-1500.md](002-staff-structure-1500.md), and [003-staff-structure-2000.md](003-staff-structure-2000.md). This document focuses on the structural characteristics of churches at 3000 attendance.*

## Executive Summary

At 3000, the church is operating as a mid-size nonprofit organization by any standard measure. Staff count is typically 30-40 FTE. The organizational structure must be fully professionalized — not because professionalism is the goal, but because amateur operations at this scale create real harm: legal exposure, financial mismanagement, staff burnout, and ministry failure. The XP role at 3000 is functionally a COO, and most churches at this size also need a separate Director of Operations or even a CFO underneath the XP. The church is almost certainly multisite or actively considering it. The LP's role has narrowed to its final form: preacher, vision-caster, and external ambassador. Everything else runs through the XP and the team of teams.

**Confidence:** Medium-high. Data is thinner at this size (fewer churches operate here), and the variation between churches increases significantly. Structural principles are well-grounded; specific staff maps are more context-dependent.

## 1. Staffing at 3000

### Staff Count

| Ratio | FTE Staff | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| 1:76 (Vanderbloemen) | ~39 | Upper bound; common for multi-campus |
| 1:86 (national average) | ~35 | Midpoint |
| 1:100 (Morgan target) | ~30 | Lean but achievable with strong systems |

**Recommended range:** 30-40 FTE. The range is wider at this size because context matters more — a single-campus church at 3000 with four weekend services may have a different staff profile than a three-campus church at 3000 with different ministry models at each site.

Note: One recent study cited by ChurchSalary suggests the average ratio may be trending toward 1:51 for larger churches, which would imply ~59 FTE at 3000. This is likely inflated by including part-time roles at FTE equivalents and by churches that have not optimized volunteer systems. The 30-40 range for well-run churches is more consistent with the practitioner-recommendation literature.

### What's New at This Size

1. **Departmentalized leadership structure.** The "team of teams" model that was emerging at 2000 is now the operating reality. Departments function as semi-autonomous units. Each ministry department has its own director, staff, budget, volunteer teams, and goals. Cross-departmental coordination requires intentional mechanisms — it no longer happens organically.

2. **Executive-level operations roles.** A single Director of Operations may no longer be sufficient. Churches at 3000 often split operations into distinct roles:
   - **Chief Financial Officer or Finance Director** — budget, accounting, giving analysis, audit compliance, legal/insurance
   - **Director of Operations** — facilities, security, IT infrastructure, vendor management
   - **HR Director** — full-time, managing 30+ employees with all the complexity that entails

3. **Creative/Communications department (not just a role).** At 3000, communications is a multi-person team: graphic design, video production, social media management, web development, copywriting, and internal communications. A Creative Director or Communications Director leads a team of 2-5 people.

4. **Multisite staff.** If the church has launched campuses, each campus typically needs at minimum:
   - Campus Pastor (primary shepherd and on-site leader)
   - Worship Leader
   - Children's Ministry Coordinator
   - Administrative/operations support
   Additional roles (student ministry, groups, care) depend on campus size and the extent of central services support.

5. **Specialized pastoral roles.** At 3000, the ministry breadth often includes roles not present at smaller sizes:
   - Counseling Pastor with a team (or referral network)
   - Missions Pastor (full-time, not combined)
   - Young Adults / College Pastor
   - Family Ministry Pastor (bridging children's and students)
   - Assimilation / Next Steps Director

### Staff Map at 3000 (Single Campus)

| Cluster | Department | Key Roles | Reports To |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Executive** | Senior Leadership | Lead Pastor, Executive Pastor | LP → Board; XP → LP |
| **Ministry** | Worship & Production | Worship Pastor, Production Director, A/V staff | XP |
| | Children's Ministry | Children's Director, Coordinators (2-3) | XP |
| | Student Ministry | Student Pastor, Associate (1-2) | XP |
| | Groups & Discipleship | Groups Director, possibly Associate | XP |
| | Outreach & Missions | Missions Pastor | XP |
| | Care & Counseling | Care Pastor, Counseling staff/lay team | XP |
| | Young Adults / College | Young Adults Pastor | XP |
| | Assimilation | Next Steps / Connections Director | XP |
| **Operations** | Finance | CFO or Finance Director, Accountant/Bookkeeper | XP or Dir. of Ops |
| | HR | HR Director | XP or Dir. of Ops |
| | Facilities | Facilities Director, Maintenance staff (2-3) | Dir. of Ops |
| | IT | IT Director, possibly support staff | Dir. of Ops |

| **Communications** | Creative & Comms | Creative Director, Graphic Designer, Social Media, Video | XP |

### Staff Map at 3000 (Multisite — 3 Campuses)

In a multisite model, the central staff handles strategy, creative, finance, HR, IT, and communications. Each campus has a lean local team:

**Central Staff (~20-25 FTE):**
- LP, XP, Director of Operations
- Worship/Creative Director, Production team
- Communications team
- Children's Ministry Director (strategy/curriculum)
- Student Ministry Director (strategy)
- Groups Director
- Missions Director
- Care/Counseling Director
- Finance, HR, IT, Facilities leadership
- Admin and executive support

**Per Campus (~5-7 FTE):**
- Campus Pastor
- Worship Leader
- Children's Coordinator
- Student Ministry Coordinator (may be part-time)
- Admin/Operations support
- Possibly a Groups Coordinator

Total: ~35-46 FTE across three campuses.

## 2. How the LP/XP Relationship Evolves at 3000

### The LP at 3000

The LP at 3000 attendance has completed the role-narrowing trajectory Keller describes. The LP is now:

- **Preacher.** This is the primary job. Sermon preparation consumes a significant portion of the LP's week. At many churches this size, preaching becomes a "team sport" — researchers, content assistants, and collaborative planning teams support the LP.
- **Vision-caster.** The LP sets and communicates the long-term direction. This is done at major moments (annual vision casting, staff retreats, strategic planning cycles), not in weekly tactical meetings.
- **External ambassador.** Community relationships, denominational engagement, industry conferences, and public-facing opportunities.
- **Mentor to the XP.** The LP's most important staff relationship. At 3000, the LP may only have 2-3 direct relationships on staff: the XP, possibly a Teaching Associate, and possibly the board chair.

*What the LP is not doing at 3000:* attending department meetings, approving routine expenditures, directly managing any staff beyond the XP and perhaps one or two others, responding to operational questions, or doing pastoral care except for significant moments.

### The XP at 3000

The XP at 3000 is functionally the Chief Operating Officer of a mid-size nonprofit. This is true regardless of which of the three XP models the church officially names. The scope:

- **Staff oversight.** 30-40 employees, either directly or through Tier 2 leaders. This includes hiring, termination, compensation, performance reviews, and culture — much of it executed through the HR Director and department leads, but the XP sets the standards and makes the final calls.
- **Ministry strategy.** Ensuring all ministry departments align with the church's mission and strategic plan. The XP is the person who can see across all departments and identify duplication, gaps, or misalignment.
- **Budget authority.** The XP and CFO/Finance Director build and manage the budget. The XP makes trade-off decisions when resources are constrained.
- **Board support.** The XP may present operational and financial reports to the board, freeing the LP to focus board time on vision and direction.
- **Multisite coordination.** If multisite, the XP ensures alignment across campuses — either directly or through a Director of Multisite who reports to the XP.

### The Critical Question: Does the XP Need an XP?

At 3000, a real risk emerges: the XP role itself becomes unsustainable. The scope is enormous. Some churches at this size address this by:

- **Adding an Associate XP or Chief of Staff** who handles day-to-day staff coordination, freeing the XP to focus on strategy and senior-level decisions
- **Elevating the Director of Operations to co-equal status** with the XP, splitting the church's leadership into two parallel tracks: Ministry (under the XP) and Operations (under the Director of Operations), both reporting to the LP
- **Creating a Senior Leadership Team** of 3-4 people (XP, Dir. of Ops, Senior Ministry Director) who share executive responsibility

There is no single "right" answer. The evidence suggests that by 3000, the XP role as a single person managing everything below the LP is reaching its structural limit — just as the LP's hub-and-spokes model broke at 1800.

## 3. Structural Characteristics at 3000

### Decision-Making: Decentralized by Necessity

At 3000, the volume and speed of decisions exceed what any single leader or pair of leaders can process. Department directors must have genuine authority to:

- Hire and manage within their department (with HR support)
- Spend within their approved budget
- Design and modify ministry programs
- Manage their volunteer teams
- Set schedules and rhythms

The XP's role shifts from *making* decisions to *setting the framework* within which decisions are made — and intervening only when alignment breaks.

### Communication: Formal and Redundant

Keller's principle of "increasing redundancy of communication" reaches its peak at this size. Churches at 3000 need:

- A formal internal communication system (staff intranet, Slack/Teams, shared documents)
- Regular all-staff meetings (monthly or biweekly) for alignment and culture
- Department-level meetings (weekly) for execution
- Executive team meetings (weekly) for strategy and coordination
- Written communication of major decisions, not just verbal announcements

### Performance Management: Non-Negotiable

With 30-40 employees, informal performance feedback is insufficient and potentially legally dangerous. Churches at 3000 need:

- Written job descriptions for every position
- Annual performance reviews with documented goals (SMART or OKR framework)
- Regular 1:1 check-ins between managers and direct reports
- A defined process for performance improvement and, if necessary, termination
- Documented policies for compensation, benefits, time off, and workplace conduct

This is not optional professionalization — it is organizational hygiene at scale. The unSeminary research identifies performance management systems as one of the five characteristics that distinguish staff teams that can sustain at or above 1000. At 3000, it is foundational.

## 4. Key Risks at 3000

1. **The church loses its soul.** The single most common criticism of churches at this size is that operational excellence crowds out spiritual vitality — that the organization becomes "more business than church." This is a real risk, not a strawman. The countermeasure is not to reject professionalization (that is not an option at this scale) but to ensure that the LP, XP, and senior leadership team are relentlessly anchoring every system, hire, and decision back to the church's mission. Systems exist to serve people and mission, full stop.

2. **Executive isolation.** At 3000, the LP and XP can become so removed from the congregation and frontline staff that they lose touch with reality. Structured listening mechanisms matter — regular time in the lobby, staff skip-level meetings, congregation feedback loops, and honest Tier 2 leaders who surface problems rather than filtering them out.

3. **Silo warfare.** With departmentalized structure comes departmental competition — for budget, for staff, for the XP's attention. The leadership team meeting and the XP's role as cross-departmental integrator are the primary counterweights. If departments cannot articulate how their work serves the church's shared mission, the structure has drifted.

4. **Over-extending multisite.** Each campus has a real operational cost. A new campus of 200 that is not self-sustaining in giving will drain the central budget. The Unstuck Group's ratio (1.25 FTE per 200 campus attendees, after central services costs) is a useful planning tool. Launching campuses faster than the giving base can sustain them is the most common financial mistake for churches at this stage.

5. **Succession vulnerability.** Keller identifies leadership succession as the "Achilles' heel" of large churches. At 3000, the church is deeply identified with the LP. A sudden departure — for any reason — creates organizational crisis. Succession planning (at minimum: who preaches if the LP is incapacitated for three months?) should be in place and reviewed annually. This is not a board-level concern alone; it is an operational planning concern.

## 5. What the Evidence Does Not Settle at This Size

- **Exact multisite staffing ratios.** The Unstuck Group's 1:100 central + 1.25 FTE per 200 campus formula is useful but varies by model (franchise vs. live teaching), geography, and campus maturity.

- **Whether to centralize or distribute children's/student ministry leadership.** Some multisite churches centralize curriculum and strategy with campus-level execution; others give campuses full ministry autonomy. Both models have successful examples.

- **Compensation structure for executive roles.** At 3000, the XP and senior staff are in a compensation range where church-specific data is thin. Megachurch compensation surveys (e.g., the 2023 MegaChurch Compensation Report covering 87 positions across 24 ministries) exist but the sample is biased toward very large churches.

- **Governance at scale.** Whether the board should shift to a Carver policy-governance model, remain as a working elder board, or adopt a hybrid model is denominationally driven and depends on the LP's leadership philosophy. The evidence is clear that the board should not be managing operations, but the positive model is not settled.
