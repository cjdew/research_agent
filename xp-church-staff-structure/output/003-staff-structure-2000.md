# Staff Structure at 2000 Attendance: What Changes from 1500

*This report builds on [001-church-staff-structure.md](001-church-staff-structure.md) and [002-staff-structure-1500.md](002-staff-structure-1500.md). This document focuses on the structural inflection point at 2000.*

## Executive Summary

2000 attendance is a major structural threshold — Keller identifies approximately 1800 as the point where the "hub and spokes" model (one senior leader at the center of all decisions) becomes *structurally impossible*. The church must transition from a single team to a "team of teams." Staff count should be 20-27 FTE. The XP role approaches full Second-in-Command scope whether or not it started there. The church needs professionalized HR, a defined creative/production function, and must actively decide whether to pursue multisite expansion. This is the size where the church begins operating like a mid-size organization, and the staff structure must reflect that.

**Confidence:** High on the structural transition. Medium on exact staff count. High on the team-of-teams model.

## 1. Staffing at 2000

### Staff Count

| Ratio | FTE Staff | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| 1:76 (Vanderbloemen) | ~26 | Upper bound |
| 1:86 (national average) | ~23 | Reasonable midpoint |
| 1:100 (Morgan target) | ~20 | Lean; strong volunteer culture required |

**Recommended range:** 20-27 FTE. Churches that have built strong volunteer systems and administrative infrastructure will be closer to 20. Those with extensive weekday programming or multiple service times will be closer to 27.

### What's New at This Size

1. **Dedicated HR function.** With 20+ employees, HR can no longer be a side responsibility of the XP or Director of Operations. Employment law compliance, benefits administration, performance review systems, onboarding, and staff culture development all require dedicated attention. This may be a full-time HR Coordinator or a senior part-time role, but it must exist.

2. **Creative/Production team.** At 2000, the weekend experience typically involves enough complexity (multi-service, live streaming, video, graphics, social media content) that a Production Director or Creative Director becomes necessary. This person oversees the technical and creative elements of services and communications.

3. **Dedicated IT.** Technology infrastructure at 2000 — ChMS, giving platforms, streaming, networking, A/V systems, staff devices — requires someone whose job it is to manage and maintain it. This outgrew "the tech-savvy volunteer" at about 1000; by 2000, it is a clear FTE.

4. **Associate or Teaching Pastor.** Thom Rainer notes a growing expectation that churches at this size have a second preaching voice. This may be the XP (if gifted in teaching) or a separate Associate/Teaching Pastor who provides pulpit coverage and teaching in midweek or group contexts. The Unstuck Group data shows this role becoming common at 2000+.

5. **Volunteer Development Coordinator.** The Vanderbloemen/Pushpay data shows volunteer-to-staff ratios of 56:1 in very large churches. At 2000, that means 300+ active volunteers. Managing recruitment, placement, training, and appreciation across all ministry departments may require a centralized coordinator who works alongside (not instead of) department-level volunteer leaders.

## 2. The Critical Structural Transition: Team of Teams

### What Keller's Threshold Means

At approximately 1800, the organizational model must fundamentally change. Keller's framework:

> "Once a church surpasses about 1,800 members, the 'hub and spokes' structure — in which the senior pastor serves as the hub and his staff are the spokes — becomes obsolete. Instead of being a team under the senior pastor, the staff becomes a team of teams. The power of directors and clusters of directors grows greatly. The church has become too complex for the senior pastor to closely supervise directors."

**What this means in practice:**

| Before 1800 | After 1800 |
|-------------|------------|
| LP/XP make most strategic decisions | Department directors make decisions within their lanes |
| Staff directors need approval for significant moves | Staff directors are *expected* to act without checking in |
| LP has line-of-sight into most ministry areas | LP is aware of direction, not details |
| XP can closely mentor most staff | XP coaches Tier 2 leaders who coach their own teams |
| Information flows through 1-2 people | Information flows through multiple channels |

### Impact on the LP/XP Relationship

At 2000, regardless of which XP model a church started with, the XP role gravitates toward **Model C: Second-in-Command**. The organizational complexity demands it. The XP cannot be just an operations overseer when there are 20+ staff, 300+ volunteers, multiple ministry departments, and potentially multisite considerations. The XP must be empowered to make decisions across the full scope of the church's operations and ministry execution.

Dan Reiland's framework becomes especially relevant here. The XP's three core functions at 2000:

1. **Chief of Staff** — But now managing *through* Tier 2 leaders rather than directly coaching every staff member
2. **Ministry Architecture** — Making structural decisions about what the church does and does not do, at a higher altitude than department-level planning
3. **Leadership Development** — Focusing on developing Tier 2 leaders who can develop their own people

The LP at 2000 should be spending the majority of their time on:
- Preaching and teaching preparation
- Vision casting and strategic direction-setting (with the XP and board)
- External representation and relationships
- Direct mentoring of 2-3 senior leaders (the XP and possibly 1-2 other key people)

### What the XP Must Shed at This Stage

The XP who successfully led at 1500 will need to let go again at 2000. Specifically:

- **Direct management of every ministry leader.** Some Tier 2 leaders should report through a senior ministry director or associate XP, not directly to the XP. A span of 8+ direct reports for the XP at this size signals that the leadership structure needs another layer.
- **Day-to-day operational decisions.** The Director of Operations must own these. The XP should receive operational reports, not make operational calls.
- **Being the single point of staff culture.** Culture must be carried by the full leadership team, not embodied solely in the XP.

## 3. Organizational Structure at 2000

### Recommended: Four Functional Clusters

The three-tier model from 1200-1500 evolves into cluster-based organization:

**Cluster 1: Executive (LP + XP)**
- Strategic direction, vision alignment, board relationship, senior staffing

**Cluster 2: Ministry (reporting to XP)**
- Worship, Children's, Students, Groups, Outreach, Care
- Each led by a Tier 2 director with their own staff and volunteer teams
- A senior Ministry Director may emerge as a sub-layer between the XP and the department heads

**Cluster 3: Operations (reporting to Director of Operations)**
- Finance, HR, IT, Facilities, Admin
- Director of Operations may attend executive meetings or sit on the executive team

**Cluster 4: Communications & Creative (reporting to XP or a Creative Director)**
- Communications, Social Media, Production, Content
- May be organized under a Communications Director who owns both external messaging and service production

### Decision Authority Matrix

At 2000, unclear decision rights are the most common structural bottleneck. A simple RACI-like framework prevents drift:

| Decision Type | Decides | Consulted | Informed |
|--------------|---------|-----------|----------|
| Vision and direction | LP + XP + Board | Leadership Team | All staff |
| Annual budget priorities | XP + Dir. of Ops | LP, Tier 2 leaders | All staff |
| Department budget spending (within approved budget) | Tier 2 leader | — | XP |
| Hiring within a department | XP + Tier 2 leader | LP (for senior roles) | Dir. of Ops (HR) |
| Ministry program changes | Tier 2 leader | XP | LP |
| Weekend service decisions | Worship Pastor | XP, LP | Production team |
| Facilities/capital projects | Dir. of Ops | XP | LP, Board |
| Staff performance issues | XP + Tier 2 leader | HR | LP (if senior) |

## 4. The Multisite Question

At 2000 attendance on a single campus, most churches are at or near physical capacity. The structural question is no longer *whether* to think about multisite but *which model*.

### Three Multisite Models (Vanderbloemen)

1. **Franchise Model:** Teaching via video from the main campus. Unified brand and message. Campus pastors provide local pastoral care but do not preach. *Staffing implication:* Fewer campus-level hires needed; central services team handles teaching content, creative, and strategy. Campus needs: Campus Pastor, Worship Leader, Children's Coordinator, and admin support.

2. **Modified Franchise:** Consistent teaching broadcasts with unified brand, but each campus develops its own worship expression and some local programming. Campus pastors preach live 6-12 times per year. *Staffing implication:* More campus autonomy requires slightly larger campus teams. Most popular and successful model per Vanderbloemen.

3. **Live Teaching Model:** Each campus has its own teaching pastor, connected through elder governance and a global teaching team. *Staffing implication:* Largest campus teams; requires finding teaching pastors who are aligned with the LP's vision without being clones. Risk: campuses may drift toward independence.

### Multisite Staffing Ratios

The Unstuck Group recommends: if a single-campus church of 1000 has 10 FTE (1:100), a new campus of 200 attendees should have approximately 1.25 FTE — not 2. Central services (operations, communications, finance, HR, IT) serve all campuses. Only ministry-facing roles are duplicated per campus.

By the time a church reaches four total campuses, a dedicated Central Services team becomes essential (Unstuck Group).

## 5. Key Risks at 2000

1. **Failing to transition to team of teams.** The XP or LP who continues to function as the decision hub past 1800 will bottleneck the organization. Department directors must have real authority, and the senior leadership must accept having less information about and control over individual ministry areas.

2. **XP burnout.** At 2000, the XP role is enormous — staff of 20+, major budget, HR issues, strategic planning, board support, LP relationship management, ministry oversight. If the XP is still trying to do this without a strong Director of Operations and strong Tier 2 leaders, burnout is probable. Reiland: by 1200 the dual role breaks; by 2000 even a well-defined XP role requires a real support structure.

3. **Communication breakdown across departments.** With 20+ staff in distinct departments, silos form naturally. Ministry leaders stop knowing what other departments are doing. The leadership team meeting must serve as the primary cross-departmental coordination point, and it must be protected as a high-priority rhythm.

4. **Underinvesting in HR.** Employment law, benefits, performance management, hiring practices, and staff culture all become more complex and more consequential at 20+ employees. A church that treats HR as "the XP handles that" at 2000 is exposed to legal, cultural, and retention risk.

5. **Premature multisite without systems.** Launching a second campus before the primary campus has professionalized its operations, communications, and volunteer systems means exporting dysfunction. Multisite amplifies whatever already exists — good or bad.
