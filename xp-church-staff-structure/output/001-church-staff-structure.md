;/# Staff Structure for Churches at 1200+ Attendance: The Lead Pastor / Executive Pastor Division

## Executive Summary

A church at 1200 weekly attendance should have approximately 12-16 FTE staff, with the exact number depending on volunteer mobilization effectiveness and operational efficiency. The Lead Pastor (LP) / Executive Pastor (XP) partnership is the structural hinge for churches at this size — the evidence strongly favors this model over alternatives, but its effectiveness depends far more on role clarity, trust, and genuine power-sharing than on any particular org chart configuration. Three distinct XP models exist (Operations Overseer, Ministry Strategist, and Second-in-Command), and the right one depends on the LP's specific gifting gaps and the church's growth stage. The most common failure mode is not choosing the wrong model but leaving the LP/XP boundary ambiguous and failing to share authority in practice.

**Confidence:** High on structural recommendations. Medium on precise staffing ratios (varies significantly by context). High on the LP/XP relationship dynamics.

---

## 1. Staffing Ratios at 1200+ Attendance

### What the Data Shows

Multiple sources converge on a ratio between 1 FTE per 62-100 attendees, with significant variance depending on how efficiently a church uses volunteers and systems.

| Source | Ratio | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Unstuck Group (2024 report) | 1:62 | Average across churches studied |
| Vanderbloemen / Pushpay (large churches 500+) | 1:76 | Large churches specifically |
| National average (various sources) | 1:86-87 | All Protestant churches |
| Tony Morgan (recommended target) | 1:100 | "Healthiest churches" target |
| Gary McIntosh (2000) | 1:150 | Aspirational/lean model |

**Applied to 1200 attendance:**

| Ratio | FTE Staff | Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| 1:62 | ~19 | Staff-heavy; common but not necessarily healthy |
| 1:76 | ~16 | Typical for large churches |
| 1:86 | ~14 | National average; reasonable starting point |
| 1:100 | ~12 | Lean, systems-driven, volunteer-heavy; Morgan's target |

### Interpretation

The wide range (12-19 FTE) reflects a real tension: lower ratios (more staff per attender) may indicate either a high-touch ministry model *or* an over-reliance on paid staff that should be volunteer-led. Higher ratios (fewer staff per attender) may indicate either excellent systems and volunteer culture *or* an overstretched staff heading for burnout.

Tony Morgan at the Unstuck Group argues that the healthiest target is 1:100, not because fewer staff is inherently better, but because churches reaching that ratio have typically built the systems, delegation habits, and volunteer pipelines that create sustainable growth. A ratio below 70:1 should prompt the question: *Are we paying people to do what volunteers should own?*

**Recommendation:** For a church at 1200 and growing, target 12-16 FTE, leaning toward the lower end only if your volunteer systems are genuinely strong. Err toward 14-16 while building toward the 1:100 ratio over time.

**Confidence:** Medium. The ratios are well-documented but the "right" number depends heavily on local factors — multisite vs. single campus, extent of weekday programming, denominational expectations, and cost of living.

### Budget Allocation

Staff compensation should consume 45-55% of total operating budget. The Unstuck Group's 2024 data shows the average church at 54%; Leadership Network's 2018 survey shows a typical range of 46-60%. Exceeding 55% constrains ministry investment and building maintenance. Dropping below 45% usually signals understaffing or an over-reliance on bi-vocational roles.

## 2. The Lead Pastor / Executive Pastor Division of Responsibilities

### Why the LP/XP Model Exists

The data is clear on one point: most senior pastors are not wired for operational leadership. Barna research shows that 69% of pastors at effective churches have preaching/teaching as their primary gift, but only 14% describe themselves as effective at strategic thinking and action. The XP role exists to fill a specific gap — not to create bureaucracy, but to free the LP to do what only the LP can do.

Tim Keller's framework is the most widely cited: at 800+ attendance, the senior pastor's role should narrow dramatically toward preaching, vision casting, and strategy. The "hub and spokes" model (where the LP is the center of all decisions) works up to about 400. By 800-1200, it is failing. By 1800, it is structurally impossible — the church must become a "team of teams."

### The Three XP Models

XPastor.org identifies three distinct executive pastor archetypes. These are not rankings; they represent different answers to the question *What does the LP most need?*

#### Model A: Overseer of Operations

- **Focus:** Systems, finances, HR, facilities, technology, administration
- **Closest secular analog:** COO
- **LP retains:** All ministry direction; staff who lead ministry areas report to the LP or a ministry lead, not the XP
- **Best fit when:** The LP is a strong ministry leader who can directly oversee ministry staff but is weak on or uninterested in financial, administrative, and operational management
- **Risk:** The XP becomes siloed as "the business person" and disconnected from ministry decisions; ministry staff may not view the XP as a peer

#### Model B: Ministry Strategist

- **Focus:** Translating the LP's vision into concrete ministry plans; coaching and developing ministry department leaders; ensuring alignment across all ministries
- **Closest secular analog:** Chief Strategy Officer or VP of Product
- **LP retains:** Preaching, vision casting, and external representation; the XP runs the internal execution
- **Best fit when:** The LP is a strong communicator and vision-caster but needs someone to ensure the vision actually becomes reality across departments
- **Risk:** Overlap with the LP on "vision" can create confusion about who decides what. Requires extremely clear swim lanes.

#### Model C: Second-in-Command

- **Focus:** Empowered to lead in all areas of the church; the LP's full proxy in most decisions
- **Closest secular analog:** President/COO under a Chairman/CEO
- **LP retains:** Final authority, preaching, and the "face" of the church
- **Best fit when:** The LP wants to focus narrowly on preaching and broad direction and is willing to genuinely cede operational and ministry authority to a trusted partner
- **Risk:** If the LP is not truly willing to share power, this model creates the most friction. It also requires the XP to be extremely gifted across both ministry and operations — a rare profile.

### The Fundamental Division

Across all three models, the core division holds:

| Domain | Lead Pastor | Executive Pastor |
|--------|------------|-----------------|
| **Vision** | Casts the vision | Translates vision into goals and plans |
| **Preaching/Teaching** | Primary responsibility | Supporting role (if any) |
| **Theological Direction** | Sets it | Ensures alignment |
| **Staff Leadership** | Leads through the XP; directly shepherds senior leaders | Manages day-to-day staff; hiring, development, performance, termination |
| **Strategic Planning** | Sets direction | Builds and executes the plan |
| **Financial Oversight** | Approves budget priorities | Builds and manages the budget |
| **Operations** | Stays informed | Owns it — facilities, IT, admin, logistics |
| **Board/Elder Relationship** | Accountable to the board | Supports the LP; may present operational reports |
| **Congregational Care** | Visible shepherd; major moments | Ensures systems exist; directs care staff |
| **External Representation** | Primary public face | Represents internally; some external role |

### What Must Be True for This to Work

Vanderbloemen's research identifies five non-negotiable foundations for the LP/XP partnership:

1. **Trust.** The LP must believe the XP supports the vision. The XP must believe the LP will back their decisions. "Without trust, no relationship can flourish."

2. **Chemistry.** Not just professional tolerance — genuine mutual respect and appreciation. If either privately dismisses the other, staff will detect it immediately.

3. **Mutual Respect.** Each must value the other's distinct competence. The LP who views the XP as "just the admin person" will undermine them. The XP who views the LP as "the guy who gives sermons" will overstep.

4. **Public Support.** They must defend each other publicly and address disagreements privately. The XP especially needs complete backing from the LP to lead the staff effectively.

5. **Honest Communication.** The XP must be able to speak truth to power. The LP must be willing to hear it. Regular, structured communication rhythms are essential — not "whenever we can grab time."

**Confidence:** High. This finding is consistent across Vanderbloemen, XPastor.org, Church Leader Insights, and practitioner literature.

## 3. Organizational Structure at 1200+ Attendance

### Recommended Structure: Three Tiers

Carey Nieuwhof's three-tier model is well-supported by practitioner consensus:

**Tier 1: Executive Team (2-3 people)**
- Lead Pastor + Executive Pastor (+ possibly one other senior leader)
- Strategic direction, budget priorities, major staffing decisions
- Meets weekly or more

**Tier 2: Leadership Team (4-7 people)**
- Senior department heads / ministry area directors
- Owns ministry execution within their lanes
- Reports to the XP (in most models) or split between LP/XP
- Meets regularly (weekly or biweekly)

**Tier 3: Staff Team (remaining FTE and key part-time roles)**
- Ministry coordinators, administrative staff, specialists
- Reports to Leadership Team members
- Meets as needed by department

### Why Three Tiers, Not Two

At 1200 attendance, a church likely has 12-16 staff. Management research (cited in the unSeminary analysis) shows effective span of control at 5-15 direct reports, with most effective range at 5-8. If all staff report directly to the LP or XP, two problems emerge:

1. The LP/XP become bottlenecks on every decision
2. Staff feel under-supported because their manager has too many direct reports

The middle management layer (Tier 2) is the structural intervention that allows churches to break and sustain past the 1000 barrier. It is also the layer churches resist most — because it feels "corporate" and creates perceived distance between the LP and the team. Both unSeminary and Keller acknowledge this tension explicitly but argue the alternative (no middle layer) creates worse outcomes: burnout, slow decisions, and eventually a plateau.

### Typical Department Lanes at 1200 Attendance

Based on synthesis across sources, churches at this size typically have dedicated staff (FTE or significant part-time) in these areas:

| Department | Typical Role | Reports To |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Weekend Services / Worship | Worship Pastor | LP or XP |
| Children's Ministry | Children's Pastor/Director | XP or Ministry Lead |
| Student Ministry | Youth/Student Pastor | XP or Ministry Lead |
| Groups / Discipleship | Groups Pastor or Director | XP or Ministry Lead |
| Outreach / Missions | Outreach Director (sometimes part-time or combined) | XP or LP |
| Communications | Communications Director | XP |
| Operations / Admin | Operations Manager or Church Administrator | XP |
| Finance | Finance Manager / Bookkeeper | XP |
| Executive Support | Executive Assistant to LP (possibly XP as well) | LP / XP |

Additional roles that emerge as churches move from 1200 toward 2000+:
- Campus Pastor (if multisite)
- IT / Production Director (if tech-driven services)
- HR Coordinator (as staff grows past ~15)
- Volunteer Coordinator (if not embedded in each ministry)
- Counseling / Care Pastor

### Hiring Priority

The historical hiring sequence (worship, education, youth, children) has given way to more contextual decision-making. That said, if building from scratch toward 1200, the evidence supports this general sequence:

1. **Executive/Administrative support** — The most common ceiling on growth is administrative capacity, not ministry programming
2. **Children's Ministry Director** — Families will not return without a safe, quality children's experience; Thom Rainer notes this is the position in greatest demand
3. **Worship Pastor** — Quality of the weekend experience becomes a primary growth driver at 400+
4. **Student Ministry Pastor** — Families with teens need this; retention tool
5. **Groups/Discipleship Director** — At 800+, small groups become the primary pastoral care delivery system
6. **Communications** — Larger churches need multiple communication channels with redundancy; this can't remain ad hoc
7. **Operations/Finance** — These outgrow volunteer capacity around 800-1000

## 4. Common Pitfalls When Scaling Past 1000

### Structural Pitfalls

1. **The LP refuses to narrow.** Keller's data is unambiguous: by 800+, the LP must focus on preaching, vision, and strategy. LPs who continue trying to shepherd everyone, attend every meeting, and approve every decision become the growth ceiling. This is the single most cited barrier in the literature.

2. **No real middle management.** Adding staff without adding a management layer creates what unSeminary calls a "leadership void." A church of 1200 with 14 staff and no middle management has an LP or XP with 12+ direct reports — far beyond effective span of control.

3. **Part-time patch culture.** Churches approaching 1000 often have a collection of part-time hires who were added to fill gaps over time. Scaling past 1000 requires replacing this pattern with intentional, full-time positions designed for leadership and equipping, not task execution.

4. **Structuring for the present size, not the next.** Nieuwhof's principle — "If you structure bigger, you can grow bigger" — is well-supported. Churches that wait until they reach 1500 to build a 1500-person structure will plateau at 1200.

### LP/XP Relationship Pitfalls

5. **Ambiguous authority.** The most common LP/XP failure mode. If the XP's authority is not clearly defined and publicly backed by the LP, staff will route around the XP to the LP, undermining both roles. The LP must share power "in the fullest sense" (XPastor.org) — not just delegate tasks while retaining all decisions.

6. **Hiring a complement, not a clone.** Vanderbloemen's first attribute of an exceptional XP is "perfect complement to the senior pastor" — strengths specifically in areas where the LP is weak. Hiring someone with the same gifts creates redundancy, not coverage.

7. **XP over-indexes on administration.** The church begins to "feel like a business rather than a faith community" (one39.co). The XP must balance operational excellence with pastoral sensibility — systems exist to serve people, not the reverse.

8. **Cultural ignorance.** A new XP who does not invest time understanding the church's unwritten rules, history, and relational dynamics will implement changes that technically make sense but organizationally fail. Every church has a DNA shaped by "legacy events and unwritten rules that aren't immediately apparent" (Integrus).

### Growth Pitfalls

9. **Not systematizing assimilation.** At 1200, new attendees cannot be organically noticed and folded in. Without intentional tracking and connection pathways, the front door is open but the back door is wider.

10. **Underinvesting in volunteer systems.** The Vanderbloemen/Pushpay data shows a volunteer-to-staff ratio of 56:1 in very large churches (vs. 16:1 in small churches). Growth past 1000 requires a dramatic increase in volunteer mobilization — ministry must be "done by many," not concentrated in paid staff.

## 5. What the Evidence Does Not Settle

- **Which XP model is "best."** The evidence supports all three models (Operations, Strategist, Second-in-Command) as viable. The choice should be driven by the LP's specific gifting gaps and the church's growth trajectory, not by what another church does. One emerging pattern: churches that start with Model A (Operations) often need to evolve toward Model B or C as they grow past 1500-2000, because the operational complexity demands a more empowered XP.

- **Optimal staff-to-attendance ratio.** The range (1:62 to 1:100) is real, and the "right" number depends on ministry model, multisite vs. single campus, volunteer culture maturity, and local cost of labor. The ratios are useful as benchmarks, not prescriptions.

- **Whether the XP should be an ordained pastor or a business executive.** Keller notes that at 800+, churches need "specialists who can be theologically trained rather than theologically trained generalists." This suggests the XP's operational and leadership competence matters more than their seminary credentials, but the role still requires deep theological alignment and pastoral sensibility. This remains a legitimate debate.

- **Board governance model.** The research is clear that the LP should be the only staff member directly accountable to the board (Tony Morgan), and that micromanaging boards "permanently stunt growth to below 400" (Nieuwhof). But the specific governance model (policy-based, elder-led, advisory board) is denominationally driven and outside the scope of this research.

## 6. Recommendations for Mosaic Wadsworth

These recommendations flow from the evidence above, applied to what I understand of Mosaic's context: a growing evangelical church in Northeast Ohio with a mission-driven culture, an LP/XP partnership already in place, and an appetite for operational excellence.

### Near-Term (Current Structure)

1. **Define the XP model explicitly.** Name which of the three models (Operations Overseer, Ministry Strategist, or Second-in-Command) reflects the current LP/XP relationship — or which one you are building toward. Write it down. Share it with the staff. This single act of clarity prevents the most common failure mode.

2. **Audit current staff-to-attendance ratio.** Calculate your actual FTE count and divide by average attendance. If you are below 70:1, investigate whether paid staff are doing work that volunteers should own. If you are above 100:1, investigate whether staff are burning out.

3. **Establish the three-tier structure** (Executive Team, Leadership Team, Staff Team) if not already in place. The LP and XP form the executive tier. Identify 4-6 senior leaders who form the leadership tier. Everyone else reports through that layer, not directly to the LP or XP.

4. **Map the LP/XP swim lanes.** Use the table in Section 2 as a starting template. Customize it to your context. The goal is not rigidity but clarity — both of you should be able to articulate, without hesitation, who owns what.

### Growth Preparation (1200 → 2000)

5. **Hire for leadership capacity, not task coverage.** Each new hire should be evaluated on their ability to recruit, train, and lead volunteers — not on their personal ministry output. The question is not "Can this person run the program?" but "Can this person build and lead a team that runs the program?"

6. **Invest in administrative infrastructure disproportionately.** Admins, communications, IT, and finance are not overhead — they are the scaffolding that allows ministry to scale. The unSeminary research is specific: the most common ceiling on growth is administrative capacity.

7. **Build the small group system now.** Keller's research is clear that by 800+, small groups become the primary pastoral care delivery system. If this system is not strong by 1200, the care gap will widen as you grow, and the LP will be pulled back into individual shepherding that the structure cannot sustain.

8. **Plan for the 1800 threshold.** Keller identifies 1800 as the point where the "hub and spokes" model becomes structurally impossible. If Mosaic is growing toward that range, begin designing the "team of teams" structure now — decentralized decision-making, department-level authority, and a governance model that trusts staff to lead.
