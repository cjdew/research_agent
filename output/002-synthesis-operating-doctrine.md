# Synthesis: Operating Doctrine for Delegation, Role Design, and Accountability

**Document type:** Synthesis for application agent consumption
**Sources:** delegation-matrix.md (original draft), 001-delegation-framework-research.md (deep research)
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Confidence key:** [BC] = Broad Consensus | [EP] = Expert Practice | [UI] = Useful Inference | [UQ] = Unresolved Question

---

## A. Core Thesis

The executive pastor in a growing church faces a compounding bottleneck: the same person who must make high-judgment decisions is also the one designing the delegation system, writing SOPs, training staff, and monitoring execution. **The first thing to delegate is the delegation infrastructure itself** — not just operational tasks. [UI]

The original draft matrix is structurally strong. Its four-lane model, five ownership types, and tiered readiness classification are better than most church delegation frameworks. However, the matrix is an **assignment document** where it needs to be a **management system**. The research identifies three critical gaps that prevent the matrix from functioning as designed:

1. **No decision-authority layer.** Tasks are assigned but decision rights are not specified. This guarantees escalation flooding. [BC]
2. **No management cadence.** Delegation without monitoring rhythm degrades to abdication or re-absorption. [BC]
3. **No ministry-owner accountability mechanism.** The "re-home" category is aspirational without an enforcement structure. [EP]

The core design challenge is not "what gets delegated" — the matrix already answers that well — but "how delegation is governed after handoff."

## B. Major Principles

### B1. Delegation is not a one-time assignment; it is an ongoing management relationship.

The matrix treats delegation as a sorting exercise (assign task to lane). The research shows that successful delegation requires continuous calibration: adjusting authority levels as competence grows, detecting reverse delegation, and intervening when ownership erodes. [BC — Grove, Hersey-Blanchard, Management Center]

**What this means practically:** Every delegated task needs not just an owner but a check-in cadence, an authority level, and a defined escalation path. The matrix must become a living management tool, not a reference document.

### B2. Execution ownership and decision ownership are different things and must be specified separately.

The original matrix uses "Own" as a single designation. The research shows this conflates two distinct authorities: the right to do the work and the right to make judgment calls within the work. Most admin tasks should be Own-Execute with decision authority limited to defined thresholds. [BC — McKinsey DARE, Bain RAPID, Hyatt L1-L5]

**The refined taxonomy:**
- **Own-Execute**: Does the work. Follows SOP or template.
- **Own-Decide**: Makes the call. Full authority within scope.
- **Own-Execute / Escalate-Decide**: Does the work but escalates judgment calls above defined thresholds.

This is the single most important structural enhancement to the original matrix.

### B3. Readiness is task-specific, not person-general.

An admin may be Gate 4 (trusted autonomous) on inbox triage and Gate 1 (SOP-dependent) on board packet assembly simultaneously. The original matrix's "Now / After SOP / After Training" tiers are directionally correct but too coarse. [BC — Grove's Task-Relevant Maturity]

**The refined readiness gates:**

| Gate | Meaning | Management Style | Check-in |
|------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| Gate 0 | Immediate — routine, low-risk, SOP unnecessary | Minimal | Exception-only |
| Gate 1 | SOP complete and read | Directive | Daily initially |
| Gate 2 | Trained + observed (3x under supervision) | Coaching | Weekly |
| Gate 3 | Proven independent (30+ days, acceptable quality) | Monitoring | Bi-weekly |
| Gate 4 | Trusted autonomous (90+ days, edge cases handled well) | Strategic | Monthly |

### B4. Admin support and ministry ownership must be structurally separated to prevent dependency.

The original matrix correctly identifies 14 tasks that should be "re-homed" to ministry owners. The research confirms this is the single most important structural decision in the matrix — and the one most likely to fail. The failure pattern: admins begin "helping" with ministry tasks, ministry owners observe the help, and the admin bridge becomes permanent. [EP — Unstuck Group data: for every 5% more budget spent on staff doing ministry tasks, volunteer engagement drops ~4%]

**The structural guardrail:** Admins provide data, templates, reminders, and logistics. Ministry owners interpret data, make decisions, lead teams, and own outcomes. When a ministry owner tries to push ownership to an admin, the admin redirects and escalates the pattern to Chris.

### B5. The "Chris Only" boundary must be drawn by decision type, not by task familiarity.

The original matrix's Chris Only list is directionally correct but mixes two categories: things Chris must decide (hiring, termination, board relations) and things Chris currently does because no one else has been trained (routine correspondence, calendar logistics). The research sharpens this into three tiers: [EP]

- **Never delegate:** Personnel decisions, board/elder relationships, mission/vision, legal-exposure decisions, speaking for the senior pastor.
- **Delegate after Gate 4:** Policy exceptions, lower-stakes conflict mediation, major vendor decisions.
- **Delegate immediately:** Anything where Chris is the executor rather than the decision-maker.

### B6. The rollout pace must match absorption capacity.

The original matrix proposes a 30-day rollout of 65+ tasks. Research on change management in church settings indicates maximum absorption of 8-12 new responsibilities per role per month before quality degrades. The realistic timeline is 60-90 days, phased as 12-15 tasks per admin in weeks 1-2, adding 8-10 per admin every two weeks after that. [EP]

## C. Tensions and Trade-offs

### C1. Speed vs. Quality of Delegation

**Tension:** Chris needs time freed immediately. But rushing delegation without SOPs, training, or defined authority levels produces low-quality outputs, escalation flooding, and eventual re-absorption of tasks.

**Resolution principle:** Prioritize the 15-20 tasks per admin that are genuinely low-judgment and low-risk for immediate handoff (Gate 0). Accept that higher-judgment tasks will take 60-90 days to fully delegate. Front-loading high-risk tasks to "get them off the plate" is a false economy.

### C2. Delegation vs. Abdication

**Tension:** Over-monitoring kills delegation momentum and signals distrust. Under-monitoring produces quality collapse and reverse delegation.

**Resolution principle:** Match monitoring intensity to the readiness gate for each specific task. Daily check-ins at Gate 0-1, weekly at Gate 2, bi-weekly at Gate 3, monthly at Gate 4. Never apply a single cadence across all delegated tasks. [BC — Grove]

### C3. Admin Capacity vs. Ministry Owner Maturity

**Tension:** Admins are available now and can absorb tasks. Ministry owners may not have the management maturity to own outcomes. The path of least resistance is to let admins handle everything, which creates permanent dependency.

**Resolution principle:** Use the "Ministry Owner Test" before re-homing any task: (1) Does the ministry owner understand they own the outcome? (2) Do they have the competence? (3) The authority? (4) The capacity? (5) Is the admin-support boundary clear to both parties? If any answer is no, address the gap before re-homing. This is a sequenced process, not a one-time handoff. [EP]

### C4. Standardization vs. Pastoral Sensitivity

**Tension:** SOPs and checklists work well for process tasks but can be tone-deaf when applied to pastoral situations (prayer requests, benevolence, conflict, offboarding).

**Resolution principle:** Split every pastoral-adjacent task into a process component (SOP-able, delegable) and a judgment component (requires pastoral discernment, not delegable to admin). The admin owns the process; pastoral staff or Chris owns the judgment. This split must be explicit for every high-risk task. [BC — Church Law & Tax]

### C5. Control vs. Scalability

**Tension:** Chris maintaining decision authority on too many items prevents scaling. Releasing decision authority too early creates risk.

**Resolution principle:** Use the five-level delegation framework (L1-L5) to create a progression path. Start new admins at L1-L3. Advance to L4 after Gate 2-3 readiness is demonstrated. Advance to L5 only for routine, low-risk tasks with proven track records. The key insight: decision authority should expand based on demonstrated competence, not elapsed time. [BC — Hyatt, Grove]

## D. Decision-Rights Logic

### The Five Levels (adapted from Hyatt for church context)

| Level | Label | Meaning | Admin Action | Chris Action |
|-------|-------|---------|-------------|-------------|
| L1 | Execute as Directed | Follow SOP exactly, no discretion | Do it by the book | Defined the SOP |
| L2 | Research and Report | Gather information, present for decision | Package options | Decides |
| L3 | Recommend and Wait | Propose course of action, wait for approval | Recommends + waits | Approves or redirects |
| L4 | Decide and Inform | Make the call, tell Chris afterward | Decides + informs | Reviews after the fact |
| L5 | Decide and Act | Full authority, no reporting required | Decides + acts | Monitors via dashboard/cadence |

### Assignment Rules

1. **Default new tasks to L2-L3** until the admin demonstrates competence on that specific task.
2. **L4-L5 on routine, low-risk tasks** (supply ordering, inbox routing, calendar management) can be granted at Gate 1-2.
3. **L4 on moderate-risk tasks** (correspondence drafting, announcement pipeline) requires Gate 3.
4. **L3 maximum on high-risk tasks** (personnel-adjacent, board-adjacent, legal-adjacent) until Gate 4.
5. **L1-L2 permanently** on tasks where the admin handles mechanics but Chris retains judgment (background-check adjudication, benevolence approval, termination communication).

### Escalation Triggers (for every high-risk or high-judgment task)

The research identifies that undefined escalation is the primary driver of both escalation flooding (too much comes to Chris) and escalation failure (things that should reach Chris do not). Every task rated High risk or High judgment must have explicit, written escalation triggers.

**Pattern:** "Escalate to [person] if [specific condition]. Response window: [timeframe]."

**Critical escalation categories:**
- Safety/crisis: Immediate (call/text)
- Personnel/legal: Same business day
- Budget/compliance anomaly: Next scheduled check-in
- Judgment/tone question: Next scheduled check-in (unless time-sensitive)

## E. Delegation Logic

### What the Research Confirms About the Original Matrix

1. **The four-lane model is validated.** Systems/Compliance, Comms/Executive Flow, Ministry Owner, and Chris Only create real functional clarity. This is superior to the common "generalist church admin" or flat RACI approach. [BC]
2. **The Own/Support/Prep/Track/Escalate taxonomy is better than standard RACI.** Retain it, but add the Own-Execute vs. Own-Decide distinction for high-risk items. [EP]
3. **The readiness tiers are directionally correct.** Refine from three tiers (Now / After SOP / After Training) to five gates (0-4) with specific criteria and verification methods. [BC — Grove TRM]
4. **The "high-value creative" hold-back (Section E) shows appropriate restraint.** Friction hunting, shadow work auditing, and Chris load-balancing should only activate after Gate 4 on foundational tasks.

### What the Research Corrects

1. **Prayer request routing should not be "Delegate Now / Own."** It should be "Delegate After SOP / Own-Execute / Escalate-Decide" with a three-tier pastoral escalation protocol (standard, sensitive, crisis). Clergy-penitent privilege does not extend to lay staff — legal and ethical exposure is real. [BC — Church Law & Tax]
2. **Background-check admin must be split:** Process mechanics (ordering, tracking) = admin Own-Execute. Adjudication of results = Chris Only. The original matrix treats this as a single task. [BC]
3. **Morale/confusion signal gathering is classified too aggressively.** Currently "After Training" — should be "Not Yet" and moved to Section E. Requires 6+ months of relational trust before activation. Premature activation risks misreading signals and damaging staff trust. [EP]
4. **The 30-day rollout timeline should be 60-90 days.** Research consensus: 8-12 new responsibilities per role per month maximum. The original plan front-loads too many tasks into weeks 3-4, creating predictable quality collapse. [EP]
5. **Several Comms/Executive Flow tasks should be re-laned as Ministry Owner with Comms support:** Volunteer recruiting follow-up, first-time guest follow-up messaging, culture-story collection, staff appreciation (selection/tone, not logistics). These are ministry-leadership functions, not admin functions. The admin can execute communications, but a ministry owner must define strategy and content direction. [UI]

### What the Research Extends

1. **Add a decision-authority level (L1-L5) to every task.** This is the single most important enhancement. Without it, "Own" means different things for different tasks and different admins, creating confusion and bottlenecks.
2. **Add explicit escalation triggers for every High-risk task.** The current matrix flags risk but provides no operational response to risk.
3. **Add a cross-lane handoff protocol.** Many tasks cross lanes in practice (a guest follow-up that surfaces a pastoral concern; a volunteer background check that reveals a disqualifying offense). The matrix does not address what happens at lane boundaries.
4. **Add a reverse-delegation detection mechanism.** Without it, delegated tasks predictably migrate back to Chris through "quick questions," "just this once" exceptions, and admin hesitation.
5. **Add an SOP development schedule.** Many tasks are marked "After SOP" but no SOP creation timeline exists. Without one, "After SOP" becomes indefinite deferral.
6. **Add a minimum-viable cross-training protocol.** If one admin leaves, the entire lane collapses. By month 3, identify 5-7 critical tasks per lane that both admins can cover.

## F. Role Design Logic

### Two Specialized Admins (Validated)

The original matrix's two-admin split is confirmed as the right model for this stage. [EP]

**Why two specialized roles, not one generalist:**
- The 130+ task scope exceeds one person.
- The skill profiles are genuinely different (process/compliance mindset vs. communication/relational intelligence).
- Cross-training provides resilience; generalism provides neither specialization nor backup.

**Why not a Chief of Staff yet:**
- A Chief of Staff requires strategic capacity, organizational authority, and deep institutional knowledge.
- This role should evolve from a proven admin (12+ months, Gate 4 on multiple domains), not be hired for at this stage.
- Attempting to hire a Chief of Staff before operational infrastructure exists creates a leadership layer without a foundation. [EP]

### Role Profiles (Key Distinctions)

**Systems / Compliance Admin**
- Core identity: Process architect and compliance guardian
- Decision authority: L4-L5 on routine process; L1-L3 on personnel records, compliance findings, expenditures above threshold
- Key risk: Sees sensitive data (HR, background checks, finances). Confidentiality agreement required. Access restricted to need-to-know. Regular access audits.
- Profile: Detail-oriented, process-minded, naturally organized, trustworthy. Does not need relational warmth but must be discreet.

**Comms / Executive Flow Admin**
- Core identity: Communication gatekeeper and executive multiplier
- Decision authority: L4-L5 on routine communication and scheduling; L2-L3 on anything involving Chris's voice, board members, major donors, personnel, or media
- Key risk: Represents Chris by proxy. Tone/timing/discretion failures are high-visibility. Requires calibration period where Chris reviews outgoing communication before L4+ authority is granted.
- Profile: Strong written communicator, relationally intelligent, reads context well, naturally discreet. Must represent Chris's priorities without overstepping.

### Progression Path

| Period | Delegation Levels | Chris's Management Style | Check-in Cadence |
|--------|------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Months 1-3 | Primarily L1-L3 | Review outputs frequently | Weekly 1-on-1 (30 min) per admin |
| Months 3-6 | L4 on routine tasks | Review exceptions, not outputs | Bi-weekly 1-on-1; weekly team huddle (15 min) |
| Months 6-12 | L4-L5 on proven tasks; begin Section C functions | Strategic guidance | Monthly 1-on-1; weekly huddle |
| Year 2+ | Possible Chief of Staff trajectory for stronger admin | Peer-level collaboration on operations | As appropriate |

## G. Ministry-Owner Accountability Logic

### The Core Problem (Confirmed by Research)

Ministry owners in growing churches default to one of two failure modes:
1. **Abdication to admin.** "Just have the admin handle it." Admin becomes a permanent substitute for ministry leadership.
2. **Overload without support.** Ministry owner tries to do everything, burns out, drops operational balls.

The goal: **ministry owners own outcomes while admins support execution.** [EP — Unstuck Group, Management Center]

### Five Accountability Principles

**1. Define outcomes, not tasks.** For every item in the Ministry Owner lane, specify the outcome the ministry leader is accountable for — not the tasks they must personally perform. The admin provides templates, reminders, data, and logistics. The ministry owner provides judgment, leadership, and results.

**2. Establish an accountability cadence.**
- Weekly team huddle (15 min): status, blockers, escalations
- Monthly 1-on-1 (45-60 min): KPI review, development, outcome assessment
- Quarterly ministry review (90 min): strategic assessment, resource planning

**3. Apply the Ministry Owner Test before re-homing any task.** Five gates, all must be "yes":
- Understanding: Does the ministry owner know they own this outcome?
- Competence: Can they do it? If not, what training is needed?
- Authority: Do they have budget/staffing/scheduling decision rights?
- Capacity: Is their plate already full? Will this get attention?
- Boundary clarity: Do both the ministry owner and admin understand where support ends and ownership begins?

**4. Enforce anti-dependency guardrails.**
- Admins never attend ministry team meetings as the operational representative.
- Admins provide data and templates; ministry owners interpret and decide.
- When a ministry owner pushes ownership to an admin, the admin redirects ("I can prepare X, but you'll need to decide on Y"). Persistent patterns escalated to Chris.
- Chris addresses abdication directly in 1-on-1s.

**5. Sequence re-homing by ministry owner readiness.** Do not re-home all 14 tasks at once.
- Phase 1 (Month 1-2): Low-complexity, clear deliverables (KPI submission, event after-action)
- Phase 2 (Month 2-3): Moderate complexity (volunteer handoff, department launch readiness)
- Phase 3 (Month 3-4): Ongoing management required (volunteer readiness verification, training upkeep)
- Phase 4 (Month 4-6): High judgment (volunteer reassignment, fit matching, care touchpoints, first-Sunday experience design)

## H. Risks and Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: Delegation Without Governance ("Throw It Over the Wall")

**Pattern:** Task is assigned to admin. No check-in cadence defined. No authority level specified. Admin encounters ambiguity, either escalates everything (flooding) or guesses wrong (risk). Chris eventually re-absorbs the task, concluding "it's faster to just do it myself."

**Prevention:** Every delegation requires: defined outcome, delegation level (L1-L5), escalation triggers, check-in cadence, and first review date. Use the Task Handoff Checklist from the research. [BC]

### Anti-Pattern 2: Reverse Delegation

**Pattern:** Admin encounters difficulty. Asks Chris a "quick question." Chris answers. Next time the same situation arises, admin asks again. Task has functionally returned to Chris while nominally remaining "delegated."

**Prevention:** Coaching questions instead of solutions: "What do you recommend?" "What would you do if I weren't available?" Weekly reverse-delegation detection in 1-on-1s. If the same task returns 3+ times, diagnose root cause (training gap, authority gap, confidence gap, or Chris's habit). [BC — Management Center "Delegation Boomerang"]

### Anti-Pattern 3: Admin-as-Ministry-Owner

**Pattern:** Admin begins "helping" with volunteer coordination, guest follow-up strategy, or team training. Ministry owner notices the help and reduces engagement. Admin is now functionally owning a ministry outcome without the title, authority, or accountability.

**Prevention:** Structural separation. Admins do logistics, data, templates, reminders. Ministry owners do strategy, decisions, team leadership, outcomes. The boundary must be explicit and enforced. [EP — Unstuck Group data]

### Anti-Pattern 4: SOP Debt

**Pattern:** Tasks marked "After SOP" accumulate. SOPs never get written because Chris (the only person with the knowledge to write them) is too busy. The delegation ceiling is reached and cannot advance.

**Prevention:** Delegate SOP creation. Admin drafts the SOP from observation and conversation. Chris reviews and approves. This is itself an L2-L3 delegation. Build an SOP development schedule into the rollout plan. [UI]

### Anti-Pattern 5: Escalation Flooding

**Pattern:** Decision authority is undefined. Admin routes every judgment call upward. Chris's calendar fills with "quick decisions" that collectively consume as much time as doing the tasks directly.

**Prevention:** Define decision-authority levels (L1-L5) for every task. Specify explicit escalation triggers ("Escalate if X; otherwise decide yourself"). Gradually expand L4-L5 authority as competence is demonstrated. [BC — McKinsey DARE]

### Anti-Pattern 6: Confidentiality Breach

**Pattern:** Admin without adequate training handles a sensitive prayer request, personnel matter, or donor situation. Information is shared inappropriately. Trust damage is disproportionate and difficult to recover from.

**Prevention:** Confidentiality agreements before any delegation. Tiered access (not all admins see all data). Specific SOPs for prayer request routing (three-tier), personnel records (restricted access), and donor communications (threshold-based review). Clergy-penitent privilege does not extend to lay staff — this is a legal reality, not a preference. [BC — Church Law & Tax]

### Anti-Pattern 7: Single Point of Failure

**Pattern:** One admin owns an entire lane. Admin gets sick, quits, or goes on leave. Entire lane collapses.

**Prevention:** Cross-training on 5-7 minimum-viable-coverage tasks per lane by month 3. Not full competence, but enough to cover critical functions for 1-2 weeks. [EP]

### Anti-Pattern 8: Premature Complexity

**Pattern:** Framework is designed with all five delegation levels, five readiness gates, escalation maps, and accountability cadences — but no one can execute it because the foundational tasks are not yet stable.

**Prevention:** The rollout must be phased. Weeks 1-4 focus on Gate 0-1 tasks with simple L4-L5 delegation. The full framework (gates, levels, escalation maps) is activated progressively as basics stabilize. Do not teach the entire system on day one. [UI]

## I. Open Questions / Unresolved Issues

### I1. Who Manages the Admins Day-to-Day?

The matrix assumes Chris manages both admins directly. With 5-7 ministry-owner reports plus two admins, Chris's effective span of control is 7-9, which is at the upper limit of research consensus for complex roles. If one admin develops Chief of Staff capacity, that person could absorb management of the other admin — but this is a Year 2 question. [UQ]

### I2. What Happens When Ministry Owners Refuse to Own?

The matrix assumes ministry owners will accept re-homed tasks with appropriate support. In practice, some ministry leaders were hired for relational/teaching gifts and may lack or resist operational ownership. The accountability cadence and anti-dependency guardrails provide structure, but they do not resolve the underlying readiness gap. This may require role redesign, additional training investment, or personnel changes that go beyond the delegation framework. [UQ]

### I3. How Is the SOP Library Actually Built?

The framework depends heavily on SOPs, but SOP development is itself a significant workload. Who writes them? At what pace? In what format? The research suggests delegating SOP drafting to admins (L2-L3), but admins in months 1-2 may not have enough context to draft usable SOPs. This is a bootstrapping problem: the SOPs needed to delegate require the delegation to already be functioning. [UQ]

### I4. How Are Decision-Authority Thresholds Set?

The framework recommends thresholds (e.g., "escalate purchase requests above $500"), but the specific dollar amounts, donor tiers, and policy boundaries depend on church-specific factors not addressed in the research. These thresholds need to be defined by Chris before the delegation levels can be operationalized. [UQ]

### I5. What Is the Board's Role in This Framework?

The matrix positions the board/elder relationship as Chris Only, which is correct. But the board may have expectations about admin access, confidentiality, reporting, and decision authority that are not captured in this framework. Board alignment on the delegation model — particularly around financial authority, HR processes, and benevolence — may need to be secured before implementation. [UQ]

### I6. How Does This Framework Interact With Existing Church Management Software?

The matrix references Planning Center, but the integration between the delegation framework and actual software workflows (task management, communication platforms, databases) is unaddressed. The framework needs a technology layer to be operationally sustainable — otherwise tracking 130+ tasks across readiness gates, delegation levels, and escalation triggers is manual overhead that itself becomes a bottleneck. [UQ]

### I7. What Is the Failure Threshold?

At what point does a failed delegation (task returned, quality unacceptable, escalation pattern persistent) trigger a structural response — role redesign, personnel change, or scope reduction — rather than more coaching? The framework emphasizes progressive investment in delegation but does not define when to cut losses. [UQ]

## J. Practical Implications for the Application Agent

An application agent building implementation tools from this synthesis should operate on the following design principles:

### J1. The Matrix Must Become a Living Management System

The static spreadsheet format of the original matrix is insufficient. Any implementation tool must support:
- Per-task delegation level (L1-L5) that can be updated as readiness changes
- Per-task readiness gate (0-4) with verification status
- Escalation triggers attached to each high-risk task
- Check-in cadence per task (not per person)
- SOP link per task (with "needs SOP" flag for tasks still in queue)
- Reverse-delegation log (tasks that returned to Chris, with root cause)

### J2. The Rollout Plan Is the First Deliverable

Before any tool is built, the phased rollout plan must be concretized:
- Weeks 1-2: 12-15 tasks per admin, Gate 0, L4-L5 only
- Weeks 3-4: Add 8-10 tasks per admin, first SOPs introduced
- Weeks 5-8: SOP-dependent tasks added as SOPs complete; ministry owner Phase 1-2
- Weeks 9-12: Support-only tasks (Section C) introduced at L2-L3; ministry owner Phase 3

### J3. Five Tools Needed (In Priority Order)

1. **Task Handoff Checklist** — Used for every new delegation. Defines outcome, delegation level, escalation triggers, authority boundaries, check-in cadence, SOP link.
2. **Delegation Decision Filter** — Used when new tasks arrive. Routes to correct lane and delegation level via decision tree.
3. **Weekly Reverse-Delegation Check** — Used in admin 1-on-1s. Identifies returned tasks, diagnoses root cause, tracks patterns.
4. **Role-Readiness Rubric** — Scores admins and ministry owners on Competence (1-5), Confidence (1-5), Context (1-5) per task. Maps total score to readiness gate and delegation level.
5. **Escalation Map** — Visual reference for all three lanes showing escalation triggers, routing, and response windows.

### J4. Key Reclassifications to Implement

The application agent should implement these corrections from the research:

| Task | Original | Corrected | Reason |
|------|----------|-----------|--------|
| Prayer request routing | A / Own / Now | B / Own-Execute + Escalate-Decide / After SOP | Legal and pastoral risk; needs tiered routing protocol |
| Background-check admin | A / Own / Now | Split: process = A / Own-Execute / Now; adjudication = Chris Only | Admin runs checks; Chris decides on adverse findings |
| Morale/confusion signal gathering | B / Track / After Training | E / Track / Not Yet | Requires 6+ months relational trust |
| Volunteer recruiting follow-up | Comms lane | Ministry Owner (with Comms support) | Ministry work, not admin work |
| First-time guest follow-up messaging | Comms lane | Ministry Owner (Guest Services) with Comms execution | Strategy belongs to ministry leader |
| Culture-story collection | Comms lane | Ministry Owner or Chris (with Comms collection support) | Culture definition is a leadership function |
| Meeting notes/action tracking | A / Low Risk | A / Medium Risk | Sensitive meeting notes require discretion |

### J5. Missing Tasks to Add

- Crisis communication triage
- Pastoral care escalation from any channel (not just prayer requests)
- Board/elder meeting prep and follow-up (relational management, not just packet assembly)
- Annual compliance calendar management (legal, insurance, tax)
- Staff development and training coordination (beyond onboarding)

### J6. Automation/Elimination Candidates

These tasks should be automated or standardized rather than delegated to a person:
- Text/SMS management → automated sequences with admin exception handling
- Reminder system maintenance → self-sustaining architecture with monitoring
- Dashboard hygiene → monthly checklist SOP
- Tech asset inventory → quarterly review cycle
- Room booking → self-service system with admin override
- Public info accuracy → monthly audit checklist

### J7. The Doctrine's Boundaries

This synthesis provides the **what** and **why** of delegation, role design, and accountability. It does not provide:
- Specific dollar thresholds for financial delegation (church-specific)
- Named individuals for roles (hiring decisions)
- Technology platform selection (software-specific)
- Board governance alignment process (relationship-specific)
- Ministry-owner-specific readiness assessments (person-specific)

These must be supplied by Chris or determined through a separate process before implementation tools can be fully operationalized.
