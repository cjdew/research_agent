# Delegation Framework Research: Critical Assessment and Redesign

**Prepared for:** Chris (Executive Pastor / COO)
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Scope:** Deep research across executive pastor role design, delegation frameworks, decision rights, admin staffing, ministry owner accountability, church-specific risks, and implementation planning

---

## A. Executive Assessment of the Current Matrix

### What Is Strong

1. **The four-lane model is structurally sound.** Separating Systems/Compliance, Comms/Executive Flow, Ministry Owner, and Chris Only creates real functional clarity. This is better than most church delegation attempts, which default to a flat "admin does everything" model or a vague "ministry leaders own it" aspiration without infrastructure.

2. **Ownership types are well-differentiated.** The Own/Support/Prep/Track/Escalate taxonomy is more nuanced than standard RACI. It correctly distinguishes between doing the work, supporting someone else who does, preparing inputs, monitoring progress, and routing exceptions. This is a genuine design advantage.

3. **Readiness tiers are realistic.** Splitting "delegate now" from "delegate after SOP" from "delegate as support only" acknowledges that delegation readiness is not binary. This maps well to Andy Grove's Task-Relevant Maturity concept and Hersey-Blanchard's Situational Leadership model (both discussed in Section B).

4. **The "re-home to ministry owners" category is critical.** Most church admin delegation frameworks fail precisely because they turn admins into permanent substitutes for ministry leaders who should own outcomes. Flagging these tasks explicitly is the single most important structural decision in this matrix.

5. **The "high-value creative" category shows restraint.** Holding back friction hunting, shadow work auditing, and Chris load-balancing until basics are stable avoids the classic error of overloading new hires with aspirational functions before foundational competence is established.

6. **Risk and judgment ratings exist for every task.** This is uncommon in church delegation work and provides a usable filter for escalation decisions.

### What Is Weak or Missing

1. **No escalation protocol beyond "Escalate."** The ownership type "Escalate" appears without a defined escalation path. Who gets escalated to? Under what conditions? What is the expected response time? Without this, "Escalate" becomes a label without operational meaning, and tasks will default back to Chris by inertia.

2. **The two-admin split assumes clean separation that may not hold.** Many tasks in a growing church cross lanes. A guest follow-up message that surfaces a pastoral concern crosses from Comms into Chris Only territory. A volunteer background check that reveals a disqualifying offense crosses from Systems into sensitive-personnel territory. The matrix does not address cross-lane handoff protocols.

3. **No defined management cadence.** The matrix says what is delegated and to whom, but not how delegation is monitored. Without a weekly review rhythm, delegation becomes abdication. Research consistently shows that delegation without monitoring cadence is the primary failure mode (see Section B).

4. **Ministry Owner lane lacks teeth.** The matrix lists 14 tasks that should be re-homed to ministry owners, but provides no accountability mechanism. Who holds ministry owners accountable for owning these? What happens when a ministry leader defaults to "the admin handles it"? What is the escalation path when ministry owners fail to own?

5. **No decision-rights clarity within lanes.** The matrix assigns tasks to lanes but does not specify decision authority levels within each lane. Can the Comms admin decline a speaking request on Chris's behalf? Can the Systems admin reject a purchase request that exceeds a threshold? The absence of decision authority levels will create bottlenecks as admins route every judgment call upward.

6. **No SOP inventory or timeline.** Many tasks are marked "After SOP" but no SOP development schedule exists. Without this, "After SOP" becomes an indefinite deferral.

7. **30-day rollout is aggressive for the scope.** The current plan moves approximately 65+ tasks in 30 days. Research on change management in church settings suggests that staff absorb 8-12 new responsibilities per role per month at maximum before quality degrades. The 30-day plan likely needs to become 60-90 days.

8. **No reverse-delegation detection mechanism.** The matrix does not address the predictable pattern where delegated tasks migrate back to Chris through "quick questions," "just this once" exceptions, or admin hesitation on judgment calls.

### What Is Risky

1. **Prayer request routing marked "Own" for Comms admin with High risk.** This is correctly flagged as high-risk but incorrectly assigned as "Own." Prayer requests frequently contain pastoral-care-level disclosures (suicidal ideation, abuse, marital crisis). A Comms admin should triage and route, not own. Ownership should be "Route + Escalate" with a defined pastoral handoff protocol.

2. **Background-check admin marked "Own" with High risk.** Correct risk rating. However, the admin should own the process mechanics (ordering checks, tracking completion) but should not own the adjudication of results. Disqualification decisions require pastoral and legal judgment that belongs with Chris or a designated HR authority.

3. **Morale/confusion signal gathering marked "Track" with High risk.** This function requires significant relational intelligence and organizational trust. Assigning it "After training" understates the maturity requirement. This is a function that should only be activated after the admin has 6+ months of established trust with staff.

4. **Budget data assembly marked "Prep" with High risk.** Financial data preparation gives the preparer significant interpretive power. Without clear guardrails on what constitutes "assembly" versus "analysis," this can drift into the admin shaping financial narratives rather than packaging raw data.

## B. Major Research Findings

### Theme 1: Executive Pastor Role Design and Bottleneck Patterns

**Broad Consensus:**
- The Executive Pastor role functions as a church COO, owning staff oversight, operations, systems, and administrative infrastructure (Tithely, XPastor.org, Dan Reiland).
- The most common failure mode is the "delegation with strings attached" pattern, where the XP delegates a task but retains invisible decision authority, creating confusion about who actually owns the outcome (XPastor.org).
- Executive Pastors with 10+ direct reports consistently report overwhelm and recommend a tiered structure with 5-7 direct reports maximum (Integrus, Unstuck Group).
- Dan Reiland (24 years as XP at 12Stone Church) frames the XP role around three pillars: Chief of Staff/Staff Coach, Organizational Leader, and Strategic Partner. His "2X factor" principle—invest twice as much as you expect back—provides a useful calibration for delegation readiness investment.

**Expert Practice:**
- Carey Nieuwhof identifies structure as the #1 barrier to church growth, noting that churches beyond 200 require fundamentally different organizational models. The "Mom and Pop" to "supermarket" transition requires role specialization, not just delegation of existing tasks.
- The Unstuck Group data shows that for every 5% more of the budget churches spend on staff doing ministry tasks (rather than leading), volunteer engagement drops ~4% and attendance growth drops ~5%. This directly validates the matrix's "re-home to ministry owners" lane—admin staff doing ministry work erodes volunteer capacity.

**Inferential Application:**
The current matrix positions Chris as both the executive decision-maker AND the delegation architect AND the SOP developer AND the training provider. This is a bottleneck-within-a-bottleneck. The first delegation priority should be delegating the delegation infrastructure itself—SOP development, training delivery, and monitoring cadence—not just operational tasks.

### Theme 2: Delegation Frameworks and Task-Relevant Maturity

**Broad Consensus:**
- Andy Grove's Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM) framework from *High Output Management* is the most widely cited delegation calibration tool. TRM holds that management style should vary by the subordinate's maturity on the specific task: Low TRM = structured/directive; Medium TRM = coaching/two-way communication; High TRM = minimal involvement, set objectives and monitor.
- Michael Hyatt's 5 Levels of Delegation provides a practical operational taxonomy: Level 1 (do exactly what I say), Level 2 (research and report), Level 3 (research and recommend), Level 4 (decide and inform), Level 5 (decide and act).
- Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership maps four readiness levels (R1-R4) to four leadership styles (S1-S4), confirming that delegation is not a binary choice but a spectrum calibrated to competence and confidence.

**Expert Practice:**
- The Management Center (nonprofit-focused) developed the MOCHA framework (Manager, Owner, Consulted, Helper, Approver) as an alternative to RACI for nonprofits. Its key insight: only one Owner per project, and the Owner is responsible for success or failure. This maps directly to the matrix's ownership types but adds the critical "Manager" role—the person who oversees the Owner.
- The Management Center's "Comparative Advantage" worksheet provides a useful filter: keep tasks where you have unique comparative advantage; delegate everything else even if you could do it better, because your time is worth more on higher-leverage work.

**Inferential Application:**
The current matrix uses a three-tier readiness model (Now, After SOP, After Training) that roughly maps to Grove's TRM levels but lacks the behavioral specificity of Hersey-Blanchard. The matrix would benefit from mapping each readiness tier to a specific management style (how much oversight Chris provides) and a specific delegation level (Hyatt's 1-5 scale). This converts the matrix from a static assignment document into a dynamic management tool.

### Theme 3: Decision Rights Models

**Broad Consensus:**
- RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) is the most widely known framework but has significant limitations in practice. McKinsey's research identifies four specific RACI failure modes: too many stakeholders get veto power; timing of consultation is unclear; delegated decisions get escalated back up; and ambiguity generates more meetings.
- McKinsey's DARE framework (Deciders, Advisors, Recommenders, Execution stakeholders) addresses RACI's weaknesses by clearly separating who decides from who advises, and by distinguishing recommenders (who propose) from executors (who implement).
- Bridgespan's nonprofit operating model framework emphasizes that decision rights must be tailored to an organization's "must-get-right" decisions—the ones where failure would undermine the strategy.

**Expert Practice:**
- Bain's RAPID framework (Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, Decide) is used by high-performing nonprofits to clarify that exactly one person has the "D" (decision) on any given issue. The distinction between "Input" (optional perspective) and "Agree" (formal sign-off required) prevents the RACI problem of unlimited consulted parties.

**Inferential Application:**
The current matrix's ownership types (Own, Support, Prep, Track, Escalate) are execution-focused, not decision-focused. Adding a decision-authority layer—specifying who can decide within each task, and at what threshold they must escalate—would close the biggest operational gap. For example: "The Comms admin Owns inbox triage and can decide routing for standard requests (Level 4: Decide and Inform). Requests involving board members, donors above $X, or personnel concerns must be escalated to Chris (Level 2: Research and Report)."

### Theme 4: Church-Specific Risk Landscape

**Broad Consensus:**
- Prayer request confidentiality carries both ethical and legal weight. Clergy-penitent privilege exists in all 50 states but does not extend to lay staff. Information shared with an admin processing prayer requests is not legally privileged. Churches face increasing lawsuit risk from unauthorized disclosure of health, marital, or personal information shared in prayer contexts (Church Law & Tax, AG News).
- Volunteer screening is a legal and insurance requirement, not just a best practice. Background checks should include criminal registry, sex offender registry, and child abuse registry searches. The two-person rule (no adult alone with a child) is now an insurance standard. Screening must be repeated every 3-5 years (Checkr, Protect My Ministry, Church Law & Tax).
- Benevolence fund management requires written policies, separation of duties (evaluator, approver, and disburser should be different people), direct payment to vendors (never cash to individuals), and documentation of every disbursement (CapinCrouse, Church Law & Tax, XPastor.org).
- Donor information access must be explicitly restricted by policy. Not all staff should see giving records. Board members have fiduciary confidentiality obligations that can be waived by courts if breached (Smart Church Management, Church Law & Tax).
- Church offboarding requires same-day access revocation for terminations, including physical keys/cards, email, cloud services, database access, and social media accounts. Ministry-specific offboarding adds pastoral care dimensions (exit interviews, prayer, dignity in transition) that corporate checklists miss (HR Ministry Solutions, StrongDM).

### Theme 5: Nonprofit Operations Scaling

**Broad Consensus:**
- The nonprofit lifecycle model (Social Impact Architects) identifies a common failure: programs reach "growth stage" while operations remain in "start-up stage." This creates the exact tension present in this matrix—a sophisticated task inventory running on immature operational infrastructure.
- Bridgespan emphasizes that operating model design must start from "must-get-right" capabilities, not from an exhaustive task list. The risk of a 130+ task matrix is that it becomes a compliance document rather than a management tool.
- Span of control research consistently recommends 5-7 direct reports for complex/senior roles. With two admin reports plus multiple ministry owners, Chris's effective span of control depends entirely on how well the ministry owner lane functions.
- Stanford Social Innovation Review cautions against scaling before "earning the right"—organizations that grow faster than their operational infrastructure can support experience burnout, quality collapse, and mission drift.

### Theme 6: Reverse Delegation and Admin Dependency

**Broad Consensus:**
- Reverse delegation (upward delegation) is identified across management literature as one of the most significant drains on leadership effectiveness. The pattern: a task is delegated, the delegate encounters difficulty, and the task migrates back to the leader through "quick questions" or requests for approval.
- The primary countermeasure is coaching questions rather than solutions. When a team member brings a problem back, the leader asks: "What do you recommend?" "What options have you considered?" "What would you do if I weren't available?"
- The Management Center's "Delegation Boomerang" concept addresses this directly: when a delegated task comes back, the manager must resist re-absorbing it and instead invest in the delegate's capacity to handle it.
- Clear "done" definitions at the point of delegation are the strongest preventive measure. Ambiguous delegation invites reverse delegation.

## C. Stress Test of the Current Matrix

### Lane Design

**Strength:** The four-lane model is cleaner than most church delegation structures. The Systems/Comms split creates genuine specialization rather than a generalist "church admin" role.

**Weakness:** The lanes are defined by function (what type of work) rather than by decision complexity (what level of judgment). This means a low-judgment Systems task (supply ordering) and a high-judgment Systems task (budget data assembly) sit in the same lane with different readiness tiers but no differentiated management approach. Consider adding a secondary axis: routine/standard versus judgment/exception.

**Stress Point:** The Ministry Owner lane is structurally dependent on ministry leaders who may not have the management maturity to own outcomes. The lane is aspirational in churches where ministry leaders were hired for relational/teaching gifts, not operational competence. The matrix needs a "Ministry Owner Readiness Assessment" before tasks can actually be re-homed.

### Task Classification

**Overly Aggressive "Delegate Now" Items:**
- "Prayer request routing" (High risk) — should be "After SOP" with a defined pastoral escalation protocol
- "Background-check admin" (High risk) — process can delegate now, but adjudication cannot
- "Volunteer paperwork tracking" (High risk) — process can delegate now, but compliance verification needs defined checkpoints

**Potentially Under-Classified Items:**
- "Meeting notes and action-item tracking" is marked Low risk but carries Moderate risk in practice. Meeting notes from sensitive discussions (personnel, budget, board) contain information that requires discretion about what is captured, how it is stored, and who sees it.
- "First-time guest follow-up messaging" is marked Moderate judgment but in practice involves representing the church's voice to people at their most receptive moment. Poor execution here has reputational impact disproportionate to the task's apparent simplicity.

**Missing Tasks:**
- Crisis communication triage (who drafts/sends urgent communications when something goes wrong?)
- Pastoral care escalation from any channel (not just prayer requests)
- Board/elder meeting prep and follow-up (partially covered by "board packet assembly" but the relational management of board members is absent)
- Annual compliance calendar management (legal, insurance, tax)
- Staff development and training coordination (beyond onboarding)

### Decision-Rights Clarity

**Critical Gap:** The matrix assigns tasks but not decision authority. For every task marked "Own," the question must be answered: "Own the execution, or own the decision?" In many cases, the admin should own execution but not decision authority. Examples:

| Task | Execution Owner | Decision Authority |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| Purchase request intake | Systems Admin | Chris (above $X threshold) |
| Candidate communication | Comms Admin | Chris (content/tone on senior roles) |
| Background-check admin | Systems Admin | Chris or designated HR lead (adjudication) |
| Prayer request routing | Comms Admin | Pastoral staff (care decisions) |
| Executive filtering/triage | Comms Admin | Chris (what gets through) |

### Staffing Implications

The two-admin model is viable but tight. The combined task load across Sections A, B, and C of the matrix exceeds what two full-time admins can absorb in 30 days. Realistic absorption rate for a new admin role: 8-12 core responsibilities in month one, adding 4-6 per month as competence builds.

**Risk:** If one admin leaves, the entire lane collapses. There is no cross-training or backup plan visible in the matrix. A minimum viable cross-training protocol is needed by month three.

### Likely Failure Points

1. **Week 3-4 overwhelm.** The rollout plan front-loads too many tasks. By week 3, admins are managing 30+ responsibilities with incomplete SOPs. Quality drops, Chris starts re-absorbing tasks, and the delegation initiative loses credibility.

2. **Ministry owner passivity.** Ministry leaders observe that admins are "handling things" and reduce their own operational engagement. The re-homing of tasks to ministry owners never happens because the admin bridge becomes permanent.

3. **Escalation flooding.** Without clear decision-authority thresholds, admins escalate too many items to Chris. Chris's calendar fills with "quick decisions" that collectively consume as much time as doing the tasks directly.

4. **SOP debt.** Tasks marked "After SOP" accumulate without SOPs being written. After 60 days, the delegation ceiling is reached and cannot advance without significant SOP development investment.

5. **Confidentiality breach.** An admin without adequate training handles a sensitive prayer request, personnel matter, or donor situation inappropriately. Trust damage is disproportionate and difficult to recover from.

## D. Refined Delegation Framework

### Revised Lane Model

Retain the four lanes but add two structural enhancements:

**Enhancement 1: Decision Authority Levels Within Each Lane**

For every task, specify one of five delegation levels (adapted from Hyatt's framework for church context):

| Level | Label | Meaning | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|---------|
| L1 | Execute as Directed | Follow SOP exactly; no discretion | Invoice logging |
| L2 | Research and Report | Gather info, present to Chris for decision | Vendor comparison briefs |
| L3 | Recommend and Wait | Propose a course of action; wait for approval | Purchase requests above threshold |
| L4 | Decide and Inform | Make the call; tell Chris afterward | Inbox triage routing |
| L5 | Decide and Act | Full authority; no reporting required | Supply ordering under $X |

**Enhancement 2: Escalation Triggers**

For every task rated High risk or High judgment, define explicit escalation triggers:

| Task | Escalation Trigger | Escalate To | Response Window |
|------|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Prayer request routing | Content involves safety, abuse, suicidal ideation, or legal exposure | Pastoral staff (immediate); Chris (same day) | 1 hour for safety; same day for others |
| Background-check admin | Any disqualifying finding or ambiguous result | Chris | 24 hours |
| Executive filtering/triage | Involves board members, donors >$5K, media, legal, or personnel | Chris | Same day |
| Budget data assembly | Any anomaly >5% variance from budget or >$1K unexpected | Chris | Next scheduled check-in |
| HR record maintenance | Any document involving termination, discipline, or complaint | Chris | Same day; locked access |
| Offboarding checklist | Any contested departure or potential legal exposure | Chris + legal counsel | Immediate |

### Improved Ownership Logic

Replace the flat "Own" designation with a compound designation for high-risk tasks:

- **Own-Execute**: Owns the process mechanics. Does the work.
- **Own-Decide**: Owns the decision. Can act without approval.
- **Own-Execute / Escalate-Decide**: Owns execution but escalates decisions above defined thresholds.

Example applications:

| Task | Current | Revised |
|------|---------|---------|
| Purchase request intake | Own | Own-Execute / Escalate-Decide (>$500) |
| Background-check admin | Own | Own-Execute / Escalate-Decide (any adverse finding) |
| Prayer request routing | Own | Own-Execute / Escalate-Decide (any pastoral-care-level content) |
| Candidate communication | Own | Own-Execute / Escalate-Decide (senior roles, content approval) |
| Donor thank-you logistics | Support | Own-Execute under template / Escalate-Decide (major donors, personal notes) |

### Readiness Criteria (Replacing Vague Tiers)

Replace "Now," "After SOP," and "After Training" with specific readiness gates:

| Readiness Gate | Criteria | Verification |
|----------------|----------|--------------|
| **Gate 0: Immediate** | Task is routine, low-risk, SOP exists or is unnecessary | Chris confirms task is ready to hand off |
| **Gate 1: SOP Complete** | Written SOP exists, admin has read it, Chris has reviewed it | SOP document linked in matrix; admin signs off |
| **Gate 2: Trained + Observed** | Admin trained on SOP, has executed task 3x under observation, Chris or designee confirms competence | Training log entry; observation notes |
| **Gate 3: Proven Independent** | Admin has executed task independently for 30+ days with acceptable quality | Quality check at 30-day mark |
| **Gate 4: Trusted Autonomous** | Admin has demonstrated judgment in edge cases, escalated appropriately, and maintained quality for 90+ days | 90-day review |

Tasks currently marked "After training" require Gate 2 minimum. Tasks in Section C (Support Only) require Gate 3 before any expansion of authority. High-value creative functions (Section E) require Gate 4.

### Executive-Only Boundaries (Refined)

The current "Chris Only" list is directionally correct but should be sharpened:

**Non-Negotiable Chris Only (never delegate):**
- Final hiring and termination decisions
- Benevolence adjudication (not intake or logistics)
- Board/elder relationship management and communication
- Mission/vision/values decisions
- Salary and compensation decisions
- Any decision that creates legal exposure for the church
- Any communication that speaks for the senior pastor or board

**Chris Only Until Gate 4 is Reached (delegate eventually with proven trust):**
- Policy exception approval (can delegate to a Chief of Staff level after extended proven judgment)
- Conflict resolution facilitation (can delegate mediation of lower-stakes conflicts after training)
- Major vendor/contract decisions (can delegate under threshold with proven track record)

**Chris Should Not Be Doing (delegate immediately regardless of comfort):**
- Supply ordering, invoice logging, routine scheduling
- Any task where Chris is the executor rather than the decision-maker
- Data entry, document formatting, filing
- Routine correspondence that follows established patterns
- Calendar logistics (not calendar strategy)

## F. Admin Role Design Recommendation

### Recommended Structure: Two Specialized Admins with Cross-Training

The current two-admin split (Systems/Compliance vs. Comms/Executive Flow) is the right model for this stage. Here is why, and how to strengthen it.

**Why two specialized roles, not one generalist or a Chief of Staff:**

1. **Scope exceeds one person.** The matrix contains 130+ tasks. Even with aggressive automation and elimination, the operational load requires two full-time people.
2. **The skill profiles are genuinely different.** Systems/Compliance requires process orientation, attention to detail, comfort with data and records, and a compliance mindset. Comms/Executive Flow requires communication skill, relational awareness, discretion with information flow, and the ability to represent Chris's voice and priorities.
3. **A Chief of Staff role is premature.** A Chief of Staff requires strategic capacity, organizational authority, and deep institutional knowledge. This is a role that evolves from a proven admin, not a role you hire for at this stage. Attempting to hire a Chief of Staff before the operational infrastructure is built creates a leadership layer without a foundation to stand on.
4. **Cross-training is essential from month two.** If one admin is unavailable, the other must be able to cover critical functions for 1-2 weeks. Identify 5-7 "minimum viable coverage" tasks per lane that both admins can execute.

### Role Profiles

**Systems / Compliance Admin**

*Core Identity:* Process architect and compliance guardian. This person makes sure things are done right, on time, and documented.

*Primary Responsibilities:*
- Financial admin (AP, invoices, reimbursements, purchase requests under threshold)
- Database and Planning Center hygiene
- Background check process management (not adjudication)
- Onboarding/offboarding mechanics
- HR record maintenance (under Chris's authority)
- Knowledge base, SOPs, document management
- Access management, license tracking, audit prep
- Compliance monitoring and policy acknowledgment tracking
- Facilities admin and vendor coordination
- Dashboard and reporting support

*Decision Authority:* L4-L5 on routine process tasks. L1-L3 on anything involving personnel records, compliance findings, or expenditures above threshold.

*Key Risk:* This person sees sensitive data (HR records, background checks, financial information). Confidentiality agreement required. Access restricted to need-to-know basis. Regular access audits.

*Ideal Profile:* Detail-oriented, process-minded, comfortable with systems and data, naturally organized, not easily flustered by compliance requirements. Does not need to be relationally warm but must be trustworthy and discreet.

**Comms / Executive Flow Admin**

*Core Identity:* Communication gatekeeper and executive multiplier. This person makes sure Chris's time is protected, information flows correctly, and the church's voice is consistent.

*Primary Responsibilities:*
- Inbox triage and communication routing
- Calendar management and protection
- Meeting prep, notes, and follow-up
- Routine correspondence drafting (under templates/guidelines)
- Newsletter, email campaigns, announcement pipeline
- Website content queue management
- Guest follow-up message execution (strategy set by ministry owner)
- Candidate communication logistics
- Board packet assembly (prep, not analysis)
- Executive filtering and "Waiting on Chris" management

*Decision Authority:* L4-L5 on routine communication and scheduling. L2-L3 on anything involving Chris's voice, board members, major donors, personnel, or media.

*Key Risk:* This person represents Chris by proxy in communication. Tone, timing, and discretion failures are high-visibility. Requires calibration period where Chris reviews outgoing communication before the admin earns L4+ authority.

*Ideal Profile:* Strong written communicator, relationally intelligent, able to read context and prioritize, comfortable being a gatekeeper, naturally discreet. Must be able to represent Chris's priorities without overstepping authority.

### Progression Path

**Months 1-3:** Both admins operate primarily at L1-L3 delegation levels. Chris reviews outputs frequently. Weekly 1-on-1s with each admin (30 min) focused on calibration.

**Months 3-6:** Proven admins advance to L4 on routine tasks. Chris shifts from reviewing outputs to reviewing exceptions. Bi-weekly 1-on-1s; weekly team huddle (15 min).

**Months 6-12:** High-performing admin may begin absorbing some Section C (Support Only) functions. The stronger of the two admins may begin developing toward a Chief of Staff trajectory if strategic capacity emerges.

**Year 2+:** If one admin demonstrates strategic capacity, relational authority, and proven judgment, consider formalizing a Chief of Staff or Operations Manager role. This is earned, not hired.

## G. Ministry Owner Accountability Model

### The Core Problem

Ministry owners in growing churches often default to one of two failure modes:
1. **Abdication to admin:** "Just have the admin handle it." The admin becomes a permanent substitute for ministry leadership.
2. **Overload without support:** The ministry owner tries to do everything, burns out, and drops operational balls.

The goal is a third path: **ministry owners own outcomes while admins support execution.**

### Accountability Framework

**Principle 1: Define the Outcome, Not the Task**

For every item in the Ministry Owner lane, define the outcome the ministry leader is accountable for, not the tasks they must personally perform.

| Task | Outcome Owned by Ministry Leader | Admin Support Available |
|------|--------------------------------|----------------------|
| Volunteer handoff coordination | Every new volunteer is contacted within 48 hours of sign-up and connected to a team | Comms admin sends initial message; ministry owner ensures personal connection happens |
| Volunteer readiness verification | Every volunteer serving in their role has completed required training and screening | Systems admin tracks paperwork status; ministry owner verifies readiness before deployment |
| Ministry KPI submission | KPIs submitted on time with accurate data every reporting period | Systems admin sends reminder and template; ministry owner provides data |
| Event/service after-action collection | Debrief completed within 7 days of every major event with documented learnings | Systems admin provides template and scheduling; ministry owner leads debrief |
| Department readiness for launches | Department is staffed, trained, and prepared by launch date | Both admins support logistics; ministry owner owns the readiness declaration |

**Principle 2: Accountability Cadence**

Ministry owner accountability requires a recurring management rhythm:

| Cadence | Purpose | Participants |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Weekly team huddle (15 min) | Status on active items, blockers, escalations | Chris + ministry owners |
| Monthly 1-on-1 (45-60 min) | KPI review, development conversation, outcome assessment | Chris + each ministry owner |
| Quarterly ministry review (90 min) | Strategic assessment, resource planning, outcome evaluation | Chris + ministry owner + relevant admin |

**Principle 3: The "Ministry Owner Test"**

Before any task is re-homed to a ministry owner, apply this test:

1. Does the ministry owner understand they own this outcome? (Explicit conversation, not assumption)
2. Does the ministry owner have the competence to own it? (If not, what training is needed?)
3. Does the ministry owner have the authority to own it? (Budget, staffing, scheduling decisions)
4. Does the ministry owner have the capacity to own it? (Or is this adding to an already full plate?)
5. Is admin support clearly defined as support, not ownership? (Both parties understand the boundary)

If any answer is "no," address that gap before re-homing the task.

**Principle 4: Anti-Dependency Guardrails**

To prevent admin dependency:

- Admins never attend ministry team meetings as the operational representative. Ministry owners attend their own meetings.
- Admins provide data and templates; ministry owners interpret data and make decisions.
- When a ministry owner asks an admin to "just handle" something that is in the Ministry Owner lane, the admin redirects: "I can prepare X for you, but you'll need to make the call on Y." If the pattern persists, the admin escalates to Chris.
- Chris addresses ministry owner abdication in 1-on-1s directly: "I notice you've been routing [task] to [admin]. That outcome belongs to you. What support do you need to own it?"

**Principle 5: Graduated Re-Homing**

Do not re-home all 14 Ministry Owner tasks simultaneously. Sequence by ministry owner readiness:

| Phase | Tasks to Re-Home | Criteria |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| Phase 1 (Month 1-2) | Ministry KPI submission, Event after-action collection | Low complexity, clear deliverable |
| Phase 2 (Month 2-3) | Volunteer handoff coordination, Department readiness for launches | Moderate complexity, requires some process design |
| Phase 3 (Month 3-4) | Volunteer readiness verification, Volunteer first-30-day check-ins, Team-specific training upkeep | Higher complexity, requires ongoing management |
| Phase 4 (Month 4-6) | Volunteer reassignment, Volunteer fit matching, Volunteer care touchpoints, First-Sunday experience design | High judgment, requires mature ministry leadership |

## H. Risk Controls

### 1. Confidential Matters

**Prayer Requests:**
- Create a "Prayer Request Routing SOP" that categorizes requests into three tiers:
  - **Tier 1 (Standard):** General prayer needs. Admin routes to prayer team. No special handling.
  - **Tier 2 (Sensitive):** Health details, family struggles, financial hardship. Admin routes to pastoral staff with "confidential" flag. Not shared with general prayer team without permission.
  - **Tier 3 (Crisis):** Any mention of self-harm, abuse, legal matters, or imminent danger. Admin immediately escalates to pastoral staff and Chris. Same-day response required.
- All prayer request data access restricted to Comms admin and pastoral staff only. Not visible to general volunteers.
- Written consent required before sharing any health information in public prayer lists (bulletin, announcements).

**Personnel Matters:**
- All HR records maintained by Systems admin under restricted access. No ministry owner or general staff access.
- Personnel discussions in meetings: notes marked "CONFIDENTIAL - RESTRICTED DISTRIBUTION" and stored in locked/encrypted location.
- Termination-related documents accessible only to Chris and Systems admin. Never stored in shared drives.

### 2. People Issues (HR, Discipline, Conflict)

- Systems admin handles process mechanics only: scheduling meetings, tracking documentation, maintaining files.
- All substantive people decisions (hiring, firing, discipline, conflict resolution) remain with Chris.
- Admin never communicates discipline, termination, or personnel decisions. That is always Chris or a designated pastoral leader.
- For conflict situations: admin may schedule meetings and prepare background memos, but never mediates or communicates decisions.

### 3. Donor and Board Communications

- **Donor communications:** Admin can execute templated thank-you communications. Any personalized communication to donors above a defined threshold ($5,000 suggested) requires Chris's review and approval.
- **Board communications:** Admin can handle scheduling and logistics. All substantive board communications (agendas, reports, strategic updates) are drafted by Chris or reviewed by Chris before sending. Admin assembles; Chris approves.
- **Donor giving data:** Restricted to Chris, finance staff, and designated admin with confidentiality agreement. Not visible to ministry leaders or general staff.

### 4. Volunteer Screening

- Background check process: Systems admin orders and tracks. Results reviewed by Chris or designated HR authority. Admin sees completion status, not results.
- Disqualification decisions made by Chris with legal counsel as needed. Never by admin alone.
- Screening records stored separately from general volunteer files. Access restricted.
- Screening renewal calendar maintained by Systems admin. Automated reminders 60 days before expiration.
- Child protection policy compliance (two-person rule, training requirements) tracked by Systems admin; ministry owners responsible for implementation.

### 5. Benevolence Program

- Application intake and documentation: Systems admin.
- Need assessment and recommendation: Designated pastoral staff or benevolence committee.
- Approval: Chris (for amounts above policy threshold) or benevolence committee (within policy limits).
- Disbursement: Finance/accounting. Never the same person who approves.
- All payments direct to vendors. No cash disbursements.
- Documentation maintained for every request regardless of outcome.
- Benevolence decisions never linked to giving history.

### 6. Policy Exceptions

- All policy exceptions documented with: the policy being excepted, the reason, who approved, the scope and duration of the exception.
- Systems admin logs exceptions. Chris approves.
- Quarterly exception review to identify patterns that suggest the policy needs updating rather than excepting.

### 7. Compliance Drift Prevention

- Annual compliance calendar maintained by Systems admin covering: insurance renewals, background check renewals, financial review/audit, legal filings, policy reviews, board governance requirements.
- Quarterly compliance check-in (30 min) between Chris and Systems admin to review status of all compliance items.
- Any compliance gap discovered is treated as a priority escalation, not a routine item.

## J. Practical Tools

### Tool 1: Delegation Decision Filter

Use this filter for every new task or request that lands on your plate:

```
DELEGATION DECISION FILTER

Step 1: Does this require MY unique judgment, relationships, or authority?
  YES → Chris Only. Do it.
  NO → Continue to Step 2.

Step 2: Does this involve confidential/sensitive information?
  YES → Can it be split into a process part (delegable) and a judgment part (Chris Only)?
    YES → Delegate the process. Keep the judgment.
    NO → Chris Only for now. Revisit at Gate 4.
  NO → Continue to Step 3.

Step 3: Does an SOP exist for this task?
  YES → Delegate at appropriate level. Continue to Step 4.
  NO → Is this task recurring (will happen again)?
    YES → Write the SOP first, then delegate. (Or delegate SOP creation.)
    NO → Handle it once. If it recurs, create SOP.

Step 4: Which lane does this belong to?
  - Records, data, compliance, backend process → Systems/Compliance Admin
  - Communication, scheduling, information flow → Comms/Executive Flow Admin
  - Ministry results, team leadership, volunteer management → Ministry Owner
  - Unclear → Default to the admin whose skill profile best fits. Revisit lane assignment in weekly review.

Step 5: What delegation level is appropriate?
  - Admin is new to this task → L1-L2 (directed or research/report)
  - Admin has done this task before with acceptable quality → L3-L4 (recommend or decide/inform)
  - Admin has proven track record on this task → L5 (decide and act)

Step 6: What are the escalation triggers?
  Define: "Escalate to me if [specific conditions]."
  If you cannot define specific conditions, you are not ready to delegate this task.
```

### Tool 2: Role-Readiness Rubric

Rate each admin (or ministry owner) on each task using this rubric:

```
ROLE-READINESS RUBRIC

COMPETENCE (Can they do it?)
1 - No experience or training on this task
2 - Has been briefed but has not executed
3 - Has executed under supervision with acceptable results
4 - Has executed independently with consistent quality
5 - Has handled edge cases and exceptions with sound judgment

CONFIDENCE (Will they do it without excessive hand-holding?)
1 - Hesitant; asks for permission on every step
2 - Executes routine steps but escalates at first ambiguity
3 - Handles routine + moderate complexity; escalates appropriately
4 - Handles most situations independently; escalates only genuine exceptions
5 - Fully autonomous; proactively identifies improvements

CONTEXT (Do they understand why this matters?)
1 - Knows what to do but not why
2 - Understands the immediate purpose
3 - Understands how this connects to broader operations
4 - Understands organizational implications and stakeholder impact
5 - Could explain the strategic rationale to someone else

SCORING:
- Total 3-6: Gate 0-1. Directed delegation (L1-L2). Daily check-ins.
- Total 7-9: Gate 2. Coached delegation (L2-L3). Weekly check-ins.
- Total 10-12: Gate 3. Monitored delegation (L3-L4). Bi-weekly check-ins.
- Total 13-15: Gate 4. Autonomous delegation (L4-L5). Monthly check-ins.
```
